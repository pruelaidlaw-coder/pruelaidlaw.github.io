About this toolkit. The prompts and lenses in this toolkit are adapted from the Jisc Beyond Blended curriculum and strategic lenses framework (CC BY NC SA), contextualised for Charles Sturt University's MEA initiative and student cohort. The terminology has been updated throughout: ePortfolio replaces PebblePad, casual staff replaces sessional staff, and course-level assessment replaces programmatic assessment.
MEA Model Definitions

Three models.
Three design logics.

The MEA models are not administrative categories — each has a distinctive pedagogical logic that flows through every design decision about place, platform, pace, assessment, and support.

Model overview

What makes each model distinctive

Each model has a core design commitment. Understanding that commitment — not just the compliance standards — is what enables teachers to make good design decisions within it.

Fully Online
Online study that meets you where you are and supports where you're going.
Design logic: flexibility through asynchronous dominance

Everything about this model flows from one commitment: that time and place should never be barriers to learning. The whole course — including assessment — is designed to work without campus attendance, for students studying in regional towns, workplaces, and different time zones.

  • Intentional online pedagogy (Community of Inquiry)
  • ≥85% asynchronous learning activity
  • Zero mandatory campus attendance
  • Mandatory online orientation before Week 1
  • Active teaching presence every week
  • Assessments completable fully online
  • Local and virtual WIL pathways
  • ≥90% query response within 3 business days
Combined
Learning designed around you, in the settings where learning works best.
Design logic: intentional phase sequencing

Not a halfway house between the other two — a deliberately engineered alternation where each phase has a distinct pedagogical purpose. Online phases build knowledge and flexibility; on-campus phases validate, apply, and connect. The design challenge is the transition between phases.

  • Intentional combined pedagogy across phases
  • Structured synchronous and asynchronous learning
  • Purposeful, informed campus attendance
  • Orientation covering both phases and transitions
  • Teaching presence maintained across phases
  • Integrated assessment — online and on-campus
  • Phase-aligned WIL pathways
  • Continuity of support across phases
In-Person Immersive
A connected learning experience — you have to be there.
Design logic: depth through co-presence

This model bets that being in the same place at the same time creates learning that can't be replicated digitally — labs, clinics, spontaneous dialogue, peer culture, belonging. Online components exist only to support what happens on campus, not to be a mode in their own right.

  • Intentional campus-based pedagogy
  • ≥75% campus-based synchronous activity
  • Immersive campus learning experience
  • On-campus orientation (or approved equivalent)
  • Active teaching presence in all sessions
  • Campus-based and supervised assessment
  • In-person WIL preparation and debrief
  • Clearly published campus support services
Jisc six pillars

How the pillars work differently in each model

The six Jisc pillars apply to all three models — but the design choices they require are fundamentally different. This is what makes the models genuinely distinct, not just differently labelled.

Pillar Fully Online Combined In-Person Immersive
Place Students are everywhere — zero campus attendance required. Design for regional towns, night shifts, shared family homes. Students alternate between home and campus. Intensive locations and dates published 12+ months in advance. Campus is the primary learning environment. Specialist spaces — labs, clinics, studios — are a core resource.
Platform Brightspace must work without instructor guidance. Low-bandwidth alternatives for all core content. ePortfolio for course-level evidence. Platform continuity across phases — tools used online must be familiar before the intensive. ePortfolio bridges both phases. Brightspace is for preparation and consolidation only — not a substitute for attendance. Selective, purposeful class capture.
Pace ≥85% asynchronous. Synchronous sessions optional, recorded, timezone-aware. Assessment distributed across the session. The rhythm of online and intensive phases communicated from the start. Assessment avoids clustering around intensive blocks. ≥75% synchronous on-campus. Weekly rhythm predictable enough for students managing work and commuting.
Blend Community built online through deliberate design — not assumed. Course-level assessment maps CLOs across all subjects. Online and on-campus phases have distinct, complementary purposes. Assessment design connects both phases coherently. Online resources support campus learning; they do not replace it. Course-level assessment anchored to campus competency.
Flex Genuine flexibility for students with irregular schedules. Local and virtual WIL. Multiple entry points where possible. Equivalent pathways for students who cannot attend an intensive — documented in the subject outline, not ad hoc. Up to 25% of enrolments may be online electives. Reasonable adjustments built in for campus-based assessment.
Support Proactive outreach using Brightspace analytics. Mandatory orientation before Week 1. Response SLA ≥90% within 3 business days. Support continuity across phases. Students know who to contact during intensives and between them. Campus support contacts visible and current in all subject sites. Early low-stakes tasks in Weeks 1–3.

MEA Teaching and Learning PD Program

Not tool training.
Real pedagogical uplift.

A mandatory three-tier professional development program for all academic staff, grounded in the Jisc six pillars. Required before teaching in an MEA model course. Designed to develop genuine capability — not just compliance — and to anticipate the revised Teaching Capabilities Framework.

★
Mandatory for all academic staff prior to teaching in an MEA model course Completion provides PPDR capability evidence and is a prerequisite for MEA course go-live. Approximately 27 hours total across three tiers.
1
MEA foundations
All academic staff · ~5 hours · Prerequisite: Foundations of Learning and Teaching · Delivered asynchronously online

Establishes shared language across all staff. Not model-specific — everyone does this tier together. The goal is a common understanding of how modes of learning work, why the six pillars matter, and what the three MEA models are actually trying to achieve.

  • 1.1 Beyond blended: a new way of thinking about delivery (~1 hr)Introduction to the Jisc Beyond Blended framework. Why "online vs on-campus" is an inadequate way to think about delivery. The four modes of participation as a more useful lens.
  • 1.2 The three MEA models: design logic, not delivery labels (~1.5 hrs)What makes each model distinctive — the four-modes signature of each. Why the model is a pedagogical commitment, not an administrative category.
  • 1.3 The six pillars: from compliance to capability (~1.5 hrs)Introduction to Place, Platform, Pace, Blend, Flex, and Support as design questions rather than checklist items. Why teaching the pillars comparatively develops judgement rather than just compliance.
  • 1.4 My course in its model: an initial mapping (~1 hr)Guided self-reflection activity: staff map their own course against its assigned model, identify the key pedagogical implications, and surface questions for Tier 2. Submitted as evidence of Tier 1 completion.
2
Six pillars in practice
All academic staff · ~15 hours · Six modules of ~2.5 hrs each · AI Literacies embedded in Platform · SuES data literacy embedded in Support

The intellectual core of the program. Each module develops capability in one of the six pillars, taught comparatively across all three MEA models. Staff engage with the full landscape — understanding why models make different choices — and then deepen in the pillar requirements of their own model. AI Literacies and SuES data literacy are embedded where they have genuine pedagogical context, not taught in isolation.

Place

Where learning happens. Campus, home, workplace, community. What co-presence enables and what absence requires. Regional students and digital equity.

Platform

Digital environments as learning spaces. Brightspace, ePortfolio, Zoom, AI tools. Accessibility, equity, digital diversity. AI Literacies embedded here.

Pace

How time and scheduling work differently across modes. Synchronous vs asynchronous as a design choice. Workload sequencing and assessment timing.

Blend

Combining modes purposefully. Course-level assessment design. Teaching presence as a designed element. ePortfolio for program-level evidence.

Flex

The choices students and educators have. Equivalent pathways, diverse cohorts, First Nations cultural obligations, RPL, and the relationship between flexibility and equity.

Support

What students and educators need to engage across diverse modes. Early intervention, analytics, community-building, staff capability. SuES data literacy embedded here.

Each module includes:

Core concept (~45 min) → Fully Online application (~30 min) → Combined application (~30 min) → In-Person Immersive application (~30 min) → Reflection and design activity (~15 min, submitted as completion evidence and carried forward into Tier 3).

3
Model application
Differentiated by assigned model · ~7 hours · Includes mandatory 2-hour Educational Designer workshop · Absorbs Preparing for your Subject

Where the six-pillar framework is applied to real practice. Staff work through the MEA Course Design Prompts tool for their assigned model, then participate in a mandatory workshop with an Educational Designer from DLT. The capstone output is a ready-to-deliver artefact — subject site, phase plan, or timetable-aligned course design. This output also serves as PPDR capability evidence.

  • Focus areasAsynchronous-first design · Community of Inquiry · Teaching presence at scale · Online assessment and supervised assessment · ePortfolio for course-level evidence · Brightspace site design to standard
  • ActivitiesReview Brightspace site against Fully Online model checklist (Place, Platform, Pace, Blend, Flex, Support) · Design or refine at least two subjects to meet the asynchronous dominance standard · Map course-level assessment against CLOs and confirm supervised assessment subjects · Design orientation sequence for commencing online students · Participate in 2-hour Educational Designer workshop
  • Capstone outputA Brightspace site meeting Fully Online model standards and a course-level assessment map with supervised assessment subjects identified
  • Focus areasPhase sequence design · Online preparation and post-intensive consolidation · Cross-phase assessment design · ePortfolio as a phase bridge · Intensive planning and student communication · Equivalent pathways
  • ActivitiesMap the course phase structure against the six pillars, identifying the purpose of each phase · Design or review the assessment map to anchor practical and competency assessments to on-campus phases · Develop an intensive plan including published dates, locations, and pre/post-phase activities · Design orientation content addressing both phases · Participate in 2-hour Educational Designer workshop
  • Capstone outputA phase plan covering at least one online and one on-campus phase, with a cross-phase assessment map and published intensive schedule
  • Focus areasActive campus pedagogy · Specialist space use · Campus-based assessment design · Timetable and cohort management · Brightspace as preparation and consolidation only · WIL on-campus preparation
  • ActivitiesReview timetable and session design against the 75%+ synchronous on-campus standard · Audit Brightspace site to ensure online content is preparation/consolidation only · Map course-level assessment, confirming all supervised and competency assessments are on campus · Design orientation sequence for commencing campus students · Participate in 2-hour Educational Designer workshop
  • Capstone outputA timetable-aligned subject site, course-level assessment map with all supervised assessments on campus, and an orientation sequence
